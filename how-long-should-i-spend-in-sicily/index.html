How Long Should I Spend in Sicily?
A Realistic Breakdown

Most Sicily advice starts with: “Spend at least a week.” Fine — but a week doing what?

Sicily is not a place you “cover.” It is a place you structure. The right trip length depends on whether you want one base or several, one coast or both, and how much transit time you are willing to tolerate.

Here is the honest version: under 5 days, stay focused. At 7 days, pick one coast. At 10 to 14 days, rent a car and do the island properly. The mistake is trying to see both coasts in under a week — you will spend half your trip in transit.

Quick answer

3–5 days: One base, one coast. Enough for a focused city break or beach trip. Do not try to combine east and west.
7 days: The sweet spot for most first-timers. Two bases on one coast, or one base with day trips. You will see the highlights without rushing.
10–14 days: Full island road trip territory. East and west, cities and countryside, beaches and hill towns. This is how you do it all.

Bottom line

7 days is the sweet spot for most people. Under 5 days, stick to one base. Over 10 days, rent a car and do the full island. Pick a coast. Go deep. Come back for the other side another time.

Why Sicily takes longer than people think

Before picking a trip length, understand the geography. Sicily may not look huge on a map, but it takes longer to cross than people expect. The main route between Catania and Palermo takes about 2.5 hours by car and around 3 hours by train — and that is just the two main cities.

Once you factor in smaller towns, winding coastal roads, and the realities of Sicilian bus schedules, distances start to bite. This is why trip length matters more in Sicily than in most European destinations: the wrong plan for your available days will leave you spending half the trip getting from place to place.

The most common mistake

Trying to do Palermo, Cefalù, Agrigento, Noto, Syracuse, Taormina, and Etna in 7 days. This is technically possible with a car, but you will spend more time driving than actually being anywhere. Pick a coast. Go deep. Come back for the other side another time.

3–5 days — The focused trip

This works surprisingly well if you set the right expectations. You are not “doing Sicily” — you are doing one place in Sicily, and doing it properly.

Pick one base and commit. The best options at this length are Taormina for scenery and relaxation, Palermo for food and culture, or Ortigia (Syracuse) for history and charm. From any of these, you can add a day trip or two without losing your entire day to logistics. For help choosing, see our guide on where to base in Sicily.

  • One base, no car needed
  • 1–2 day trips maximum (Etna from Taormina, Monreale from Palermo, Noto from Ortigia)
  • Best for: couples, city-break travellers, first-time visitors testing the waters
  • Do not attempt: two bases or cross-island travel
7 days — The sweet spot

A week is the right amount of time for most first trips to Sicily. You can comfortably do two bases on the same coast, or one base with several day trips. You will have time to eat well, walk around without rushing, and actually enjoy where you are.

The key decision at 7 days is east or west. The east coast (Catania, Taormina, Syracuse) has better train connections, more established tourism infrastructure, and is easier to do without a car. The west (Palermo, Trapani, Cefalù) is rougher, more authentically Sicilian, and rewards having a car.

  • Two bases on one coast, or one base with 3–4 day trips
  • Car optional on the east coast, recommended on the west
  • Best for: first-timers who want a real feel for the island
  • Do not attempt: the full island loop — you will be exhausted
10–14 days — The full experience

This is where Sicily really opens up. At 10 days or more, you have time to do a proper loop of the island — east coast, south coast, west coast — without feeling like you are on a forced march. You can stop in smaller towns, spend a night in an agriturismo, and linger over a three-hour lunch without checking your watch.

At this length you almost certainly need a car. Public transport simply does not connect the smaller towns and countryside stops that make a longer trip worthwhile. Consider a one-way rental: pick up in Catania, drop off in Palermo, or vice versa. See our full breakdown on whether you need a car in Sicily.

  • 3–5 bases across the island
  • Rent a car — this is road trip territory
  • Best for: anyone who wants to see both coasts, families, food and wine travellers
  • Consider: a one-way car rental to avoid doubling back

East coast

Catania + Taormina + Ortigia

  • Fly into Catania
  • 1 night Catania (explore the city)
  • 3 nights Taormina (Etna day trip, beach day)
  • 2 nights Ortigia (Noto day trip)

  • No car needed
West coast

Palermo + Cefalù

  • Fly into Palermo
  • 4 nights Palermo (markets, Monreale, street food)
  • 2 nights Cefalù (beach, cathedral, old town)
  • Train to Cefalù: 45 min
  • Car not needed for this specific route, but useful if adding Trapani or Erice

10–14 days — Full island road trip

10-day loop

East to West

  • 1 night Agrigento (Valley of the Temples)
  • 2 nights Trapani area (San Vito Lo Capo, Erice)

14-day deep dive

  • Drop off at Palermo airport
One-way rental tip

For any trip of 10 days or more, book a one-way car rental — pick up at one airport, drop off at the other. The one-way fee is typically 30–60 euros and saves you an entire day of backtracking across the island.

Factors that should change your trip length

Beach time: If beaches are a priority, add days. The best beaches are not next to the main towns — they require driving and half-day commitments. Trying to squeeze beach time into a packed sightseeing itinerary is how you end up enjoying neither.

Food and wine: Sicily's food scene is extraordinary, but it rewards slow days. Market visits, long lunches, winery visits on Etna — these things take time. If food is a major reason for going, budget at least 7 days.

Kids: Add a buffer. A 7-day adult itinerary can easily feel like a 9- or 10-day trip with kids. Things take longer, nap schedules matter, and you will want flexibility when plans change.

Shoulder season vs summer: In July and August, everything is hotter, busier, and slower. Add a day or two as buffer. In spring or autumn, you will move faster and enjoy longer days outdoors.

Do not try to combine Sicily with mainland Italy

Every few months someone asks if they can “do Rome, Amalfi, and Sicily in 10 days.” You can — but you will spend a huge chunk of those days just getting between places. Treat Sicily as its own trip. Fly in, fly out. You will have a far better time.

Verdict

If you have 5 days or fewer, pick one base and commit. Do not try to see the whole island. Taormina, Palermo, and Ortigia are all excellent single-base options with enough depth to fill a short trip.

If you have 7 days, pick one coast — east or west — and do two bases. This is the sweet spot where you see enough to feel like you have experienced Sicily without spending your trip in transit.

If you have 10 days or more, rent a car, do the full loop, and do not look back. This is one of the best road trips in Europe and the extra days pay for themselves in reduced stress and increased enjoyment.
